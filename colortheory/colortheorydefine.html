Color Theory Overview

Color Theory can be defined as the relationship between colors. This relationship is created in a variety of different ways such as, by mixing colors, creating color schemes etc. Explore the 20 key color theory terms defined below.

Primary Colors
colors that cannot be created by mixing other colors. These are red, yellow, and blue.
Secondary Colors
colors that are created by mixing two primary colors. For example, yellow and blue mixed equally will create green. There are three secondary colors in total.
Tertiary Colors
colors that are created by mixing a primary color with a secondary color. For example, mixing blue and green will create blue green. There are six tertiary colors in total.
Complementary Colors
colors that are opposite of a color on the color wheel.
Triadic Colors
colors that are evenly spaced out on the color wheel.
Split Colors
colors that include one base color and two colors on the opposite side of the color wheel.
Analogous Colors
colors that are adjacent to each other on the color wheel.
Hue
is the purest form of a color. There are 12 hues in total which are the primary colors, secondary colors, and tertiary colors.
Value of a Hue
hue value is the lightness or darkness of a hue.
Shades
are created by mixing black with a hue.
Tints
are created by mixing white with a hue.
Tones
are created by mixing gray with a hue.
Contrast
the differences in the colors.
Vibrancy
brightness, or vividness in the color.
Saturation
the level of brightness and purity in a color.
Color scheme
all the colors that will be used in a project in a organized manner.
Monochromatic colors
follow a monotoned color theme and are colors that are adjacent to each other on the color wheel.
Neutral colors
are colors that are soft, or light colors. For example, beige and white.
Warm colors
are colors that have warmth to them. For example, red and orange have a warmer base.
Cool colors
are colors that are calming or cooling. Blue and green are a few examples of these colors.
